What Yard Drainage Installation Typically Costs

Nationally, yard drainage installation runs between roughly $2,150 and $7,200, averaging around $4,600 for a full project. Simpler fixes can come in under $1,000, while a large property with multiple problem areas or a more involved system can climb toward $15,000. Most contractors price by linear foot, and you'll typically see somewhere between $10 and $50 per foot depending on the type of system.

Those numbers aren't a quote. They're a starting point. The real answer depends on which system your yard needs, and that's usually the single biggest factor in the final price.

Cost by Drainage System Type

  • French drains. Roughly $20 to $50 per linear foot, with most full installations landing between $2,000 and $7,000. These are the go to fix for redirecting groundwater away from a foundation, which makes them the most common system homeowners with foundation water issues end up needing.
  • Trench drains. About $30 to $70 per linear foot, typically $2,500 to $8,000 total. These handle surface water along driveways, patios, and walkways rather than groundwater.
  • Dry wells. Priced more per unit than per foot, generally $300 to $1,000 each, with projects totaling $1,000 to $4,000. These collect and slowly disperse excess water underground.
  • Catch basins. Usually $50 to $200 per unit, $600 to $2,000 total. These intercept runoff at low spots before it becomes a standing water problem.
  • Swales. The most affordable option at $5 to $15 per linear foot, $800 to $3,000 total, since they rely on regrading a gentle slope rather than digging trenches and laying pipe.
  • Surface drains. $10 to $40 per linear foot, $1,200 to $5,000 total, best suited to flatter yards that need water moved off the surface quickly.

My honest opinion after seeing a lot of these jobs get quoted: the system matters more than the brand name attached to it. A French drain isn't automatically the right answer just because it's the one homeowners have heard of. A simple swale regrade solves plenty of problems for a fraction of the cost, and a good contractor will tell you that, not just sell you the bigger job.

What Actually Moves the Price Up or Down

Beyond system type, a handful of factors determine where your project lands within these ranges.

Yard Size and Site Conditions

Yard size matters as much as system choice. A small problem area under 500 square feet might run $800 to $2,000. A medium yard between 500 and 2,000 square feet typically falls in the $2,000 to $5,000 range. Larger properties over 2,000 square feet, or those with multiple wet spots, can run $5,000 to $12,000.

Labor is usually billed at $50 to $150 per hour, though many contractors fold this into a per foot or per project price instead of billing separately. Site conditions push labor costs around, and this is where NW Pennsylvania properties tend to run higher than a national average would suggest. Dense tree roots, rocky soil, and steep grades all take more time and skill, and that's the norm rather than the exception around here. A lot of our clay heavy soil and hillside lots make for tougher digging than flatter, sandier ground elsewhere.

Site prep adds another $300 to $1,000 in most cases, covering grading, debris removal, and locating underground utilities before anyone breaks ground.

Extra Costs Worth Budgeting For

A few things can add real money on top of the base install:

  • Removing an old or failed drainage system before the new one goes in
  • Restoring the yard afterward with seed or sod
  • Permit and inspection fees where local codes require them
  • Buried utility lines, large rocks, or other surprises once digging starts

One opinion I'll offer plainly: more drainage isn't automatically better. Oversized systems, unnecessary digging, or drainage installed where simple regrading would've solved the problem are a common way homeowners end up overpaying. A contractor worth hiring sizes the fix to the actual water problem in front of them, not the biggest job they can write up.

Why Foundation Water Is the Number One Call I Get

Of every drainage request that comes through, water showing up near the foundation is by far the most common. It usually shows up the same way. A damp basement wall, a strip of lawn that never fully dries out, or visible pooling against the house after a hard rain or spring thaw.

Around here, it's rarely a mystery why. A lot of NW Pennsylvania properties sit on heavy clay soil that doesn't drain well to begin with, plus older homes that were graded decades ago and have since settled or shifted, if they ever had proper drainage installed at all. Add in a real freeze thaw cycle every spring, and water that has nowhere else to go finds the path of least resistance, which is often straight toward the house.

The good news, and something I try to reassure homeowners of every time this comes up, is that foundation water is one of the more predictable problems to fix. In most cases it comes down to a French drain, regrading, or a combination of the two, rather than something exotic or budget breaking.

Getting an Accurate Quote for Your Property

Every yard is different, and everything above is exactly that, a range, not a quote. Soil type, slope, how far water has to travel, and what's already in the ground all change the real number for your specific property. The only way to know what your job actually costs is to have someone walk it and look.
